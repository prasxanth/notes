Root Text
योगश्चित्तवृत्तिनिरोधः
Yoga is the cessation of the modifications of the mind. — Patañjali YS 1.2

Swami Paramahamsananda Sarasvatī

The teacher of this course, transmitting the Yoga Sudhākara commentary in the living oral tradition of Parampara Rishividya. Teaching in Sanskrit and Tamil with English exposition, the Swami follows the Advaita Vedānta reading of Patañjali — viewing Kaivalya as non-different from Brahman-realization rather than the dualistic Puruṣa-isolation of classical Sāṃkhya-Yoga.

Sadasiva Brahmendra (Yoga Sudhākara)

17th-century Advaita saint and disciple of Paramasivendra Sarasvatī (57th Śaṅkarācārya of Kāñcī Kāmakoṭi Pīṭham). His samādhi is at Nerur near Trichy. The Yoga Sudhākara — "Ambrosia of Yoga" — is his Sanskrit commentary on Patañjali, reading each sūtra through a non-dual, Advaitin lens. His other works include the Ātmavidyā Vilāsam and famous kīrtanas like "Sarvam Brahmaṃayam."

What makes Yoga Sudhākara distinctive
Advaita lens
Non-dual reading of Patañjali — Kaivalya as Brahman
›
Sadasiva Brahmendra's radical move: Classical Sāṃkhya-Yoga treats Kaivalya as the isolation of Puruṣa from Prakṛti — a dualistic final state. The Yoga Sudhākara re-reads this as Advaitic: the "isolation" is really the recognition that Puruṣa alone is, that Prakṛti was never separate — identical to the Vedāntic realization of Ātman as Brahman.
This makes the Yoga Sutras a fully Vedantic text in Sadasiva Brahmendra's hands — continuous with Śaṅkara's Advaita rather than standing apart from it. Swami Paramahamsananda Sarasvatī carries this reading into each class.
Avadhūta voice
Commentary by a realized avadhūta — not a scholar
›
Sadasiva Brahmendra was a wandering avadhūta — beyond social convention, often found naked in trance near the Kāverī river. His commentary is not academic exposition but the direct speech of one who lived Samādhi. Paramahansa Yogananda mentions his jīva samādhi site in the Autobiography of a Yogi.
The Swami's teaching transmits this avadhūta quality: emphasis on direct experience over philosophical debate, on silence as the deepest commentary, and on the teacher's state as the primary transmission.

Samādhi Pāda

समाधिपादः · Chapter I · 51 Sūtras

Chapter theme
अथ योगानुशासनम्
Now begins the authoritative teaching on Yoga. This chapter addresses the advanced seeker already established in sattva, explaining the nature and stages of Samādhi — the ultimate aim of all yogic practice.
Unit 1 · Sūtras 1.1–1.4 · The Gateway — What Yoga Is
Unit insight
Three sūtras contain the entire teaching. 1.1 says when. 1.2 says what. 1.3 says what results. 1.4 diagnoses the problem. Master these four and the remaining 192 sūtras are commentary.
1.1
Atha Yogānuśāsanam — Now begins Yoga
Opening sūtra
›
अथ योगानुशासनम्
atha yogānuśāsanam
Word by word
SanskritIASTMeaning
अथathanow — presupposes prior preparation; also an auspicious opening particle
योगyogasamādhi; from root yuj — concentration/union; not merely posture
अनुशासनम्anuśāsanamauthoritative instruction following a lineage (anu = in the tradition of)
Translation: Now begins the authoritative instruction on Yoga.
Yoga Sudhākara key insight: Atha is the critical word. This text is not for beginners — it is for one whose preliminary disciplines (yama, niyama, āsana, prāṇāyāma) are already established. Sadasiva Brahmendra opens by announcing that Samādhi has two forms — Samprajñāta (conscious) and Asamprajñāta (super-conscious) — before even defining yoga. The destination is named before the path.
Five states of citta
The map of the mind — where you are, where yoga begins
›
StateIASTCharacter
क्षिप्तkṣiptaDistracted — rajas dominant; chasing sense objects
मूढmūḍhaDeluded — tamas dominant; inertia and delusion
विक्षिप्तvikṣiptaScattered — occasional sattva; can concentrate briefly

Yoga proper operates only in ekāgra and niruddha states. The first three states are not yogic — they are the condition the practice aims to overcome. This is why Patañjali begins with atha — "now, after prior preparation."
1.2
Yogaś Citta-Vṛtti-Nirodhaḥ — The Definition
Core definition
›
योगश्चित्तवृत्तिनिरोधः
yogaś citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ
Word by word
SanskritIASTMeaning
चित्तcittatotal mind-field: buddhi (intellect) + ahaṃkāra (ego) + manas (lower mind)
वृत्तिvṛttimodifications; fluctuations; waves; any mental activity whatsoever
निरोधःnirodhaḥcessation; complete dissolution — not suppression (from ni + rudh: to confine until it stops)
Translation: Yoga is the cessation of the modifications of the mind-field.
Yoga Sudhākara — the Advaitic reading: Nirodha does not mean suppression (which creates rebound) but complete dissolution. When vṛttis dissolve, what remains is not a blank — it is pure consciousness that was always present but obscured. The vṛttis were never modifications of consciousness itself — only of the apparent mind-instrument. This distinction is Sadasiva Brahmendra's crucial contribution: cessation reveals, not creates. Liberation is already present; yoga removes what hides it.
"The vṛttis are like clouds. The sky was never absent — only hidden. Nirodha is not making the sky; it is clearing the clouds."
1.3–1.4
The Seer Revealed and the Seer Confused
Two states
›
Sūtra 1.3
तदा द्रष्टुः स्वरूपेऽवस्थानम्
tadā draṣṭuḥ svarūpe'vasthānam
Translation: Then the Seer abides in its own nature.
Key insight: Svarūpa (own nature) is not a new state reached — it is what the Seer always was. Yoga does not produce the Seer; it removes the misidentification that was hiding it. Sadasiva Brahmendra equates this svarūpa with Brahman — the Seer's own nature is not individual consciousness but universal consciousness. The individual and the universal were never actually separate.
Sūtra 1.4
वृत्तिसारूप्यमितरत्र
vṛtti-sārūpyam itaratra
Translation: Otherwise, the Seer appears to take the form of the modifications.
The diagnosis of saṃsāra: Sārūpya = seeming-sameness. Pure consciousness does not actually change — it only appears to take the shape of each thought, emotion, and memory that arises. This is vivartavāda (apparent transformation without real change) — the Advaitic doctrine applied to yoga. The entire problem of existence is contained here: not sin, not weakness, not karma — only a case of mistaken identity. Kaivalya (liberation) is therefore not an achievement but a recognition: "I was never the vṛtti."
"1.3 is the goal. 1.4 is the problem. 1.2 is the method. Together they are the entire teaching — the remaining 192 sūtras are elaboration."
Unit 2 · Sūtras 1.5–1.16 · The Five Vṛttis and the Twin Pillars
Unit insight
Patañjali catalogues what the mind does (five vṛttis), then gives the only two means of stopping it (abhyāsa + vairāgya). Understanding why even valid cognition must cease — and what supreme dispassion actually is — is the heart of this unit.
1.5–1.11
The Five Vṛttis — What the Mind Does
Types of modification
›
वृत्तयः पञ्चतय्यः क्लिष्टाक्लिष्टाः
vṛttayaḥ pañcatayyaḥ kliṣṭākliṣṭāḥ
Translation: The modifications are fivefold — painful and non-painful.
VṛttiIASTNature
प्रमाणpramāṇaValid cognition — perception, inference, testimony. Still a modification — must cease.
विपर्ययviparyayaError — mistaking rope for snake; world for Brahman. The root ignorance made concrete.
विकल्पvikalpaImagination — verbal knowledge without existing object. Includes much of "thinking."
निद्राnidrāSleep — modification based on the absence of mental content. Not liberation.
स्मृतिsmṛtiMemory — recollection of past experience coloring the present moment.
Yoga Sudhākara — the radical point: Even pramāṇa (valid cognition) is a vṛtti to be transcended. Even the correct thought "I am Brahman" is still a thought. Yoga requires the cessation of this thought too — because what that thought points to cannot itself be a thought. The direct recognition that remains when all five vṛttis have ceased is what Sadasiva Brahmendra calls the Advaitic completion of yoga. This is why yoga and jñāna converge: both aim beyond all mental content.
1.12–1.14
Abhyāsa — What Practice Actually Means
The first pillar
›
अभ्यासवैराग्याभ्यां तन्निरोधः
abhyāsa-vairāgyābhyāṃ tan-nirodhaḥ
Translation: The cessation of modifications comes through practice and dispassion.
What abhyāsa is: The repeated effort to return the mind to steadiness that naturally flows outward. Not a technique — a disposition. Sūtra 1.14 defines it precisely: sa tu dīrgha-kāla nairantarya sat-kāra-ādara-sevito dṛḍha-bhūmiḥ — practice becomes firm-grounded when pursued over long time, without interruption, and with reverence and devotion. Any single one of these three without the others does not establish yoga.
Yoga Sudhākara
Why time cannot be shortcut — Sadasiva Brahmendra's analogy
›
Sadasiva Brahmendra: expecting samādhi in a few months is "as absurd as saying: there are only four Vedas, yet five days have passed since my son left to study them, and he still has not returned." The depth of saṃskāras (latent impressions) accumulated over lifetimes requires proportional sustained effort to dissolve. There is no shortcut — only the shortcut of not wasting time.
1.15–1.16
Vairāgya — Dispassion, Lower and Supreme
The second pillar
›
दृष्टानुश्रविकविषयवितृष्णस्य वशीकारसंज्ञा वैराग्यम्।
तत्परं पुरुषख्यातेर्गुणवैतृष्ण्यम्॥
dṛṣṭānuśravika-viṣaya-vitṛṣṇasya vaśīkāra-saṃjñā vairāgyam |
tat-paraṃ puruṣa-khyāter guṇa-vaitṛṣṇyam ||
Translation (1.15): Dispassion is the consciousness of mastery — no craving for seen or revealed (scriptural) objects.
Translation (1.16): The higher dispassion arises from the discernment of Puruṣa — freedom from craving even for the guṇas themselves.
Four stages
The four degrees of apara-vairāgya (lower dispassion)
›
StageIASTWhat happens
यतमानyatamānaStriving — discriminating essential from non-essential, with teacher's help
व्यतिरेकvyatirekaDiscernment — separating ripened desires from those still dormant
एकेन्द्रियत्वekendriyatvaSingle-sense — only trace curiosity remains, active craving gone

Para-vairāgya (supreme dispassion): Sadasiva Brahmendra's decisive point — this is not an achievement but a recognition. When Puruṣa (pure consciousness) is directly known, craving ceases not through will but through understanding. The one who would crave is seen through. This supreme dispassion is functionally identical to Śaṅkara's viveka (discrimination between Real and unreal) — the point where yoga and Advaita Vedānta meet.
Unit 3 · Sūtras 1.17–1.22 · Stages of Samādhi — Sabīja
Unit insight
Patañjali maps the progressive deepening of samādhi through four conscious stages — each more subtle than the last. The key: even bliss (ānanda) and the pure I-sense (asmitā) are still vṛttis, still sabīja (with seed). Until the seed itself is roasted, liberation is not complete.
1.17
Samprajñāta Samādhi — Four Stages of Conscious Absorption
Sabīja samādhi
›
वितर्कविचारानन्दास्मितारूपानुगमात्सम्प्रज्ञातः
vitarka-vicāra-ānanda-asmitā-rūpa-anugamāt samprajñātaḥ
Translation: Samprajñāta samādhi is accompanied by gross examination (vitarka), subtle reflection (vicāra), joy (ānanda), or pure I-am-ness (asmitā).
StageObjectWhat the mind merges with
सवितर्कGross formConcentration with gross examination — mind cognizes the object in its concrete aspect including word and meaning
निर्वितर्कGross form aloneWord and ordinary meaning drop away — only the object's pure essence remains, memory dormant
सविचारSubtle elementConcentration moves to the tanmātras (subtle sense-essences); time and space still present
निर्विचारSubtle aloneTime, space, and causation drop away — pure essence of subtle element; this is the threshold of nirbīja
Yoga Sudhākara — ānanda and asmitā: Beyond nirvicāra, two more sabīja stages remain: sānanda (samādhi with bliss — absorption in the sattva of the sense-organs) and sāsmitā (samādhi with pure I-am-ness — the subtlest possible mental modification). Sadasiva Brahmendra warns: even this exquisite bliss and pure "I am" are still objects of awareness, still vṛttis. Mistaking sānanda for liberation is the most refined error on the path — the one most likely to trap the advanced practitioner.
"Even the experience of bliss is a vṛtti. Even the sense 'I am' is still an object of awareness. The final state has no experience, no experiencer — only awareness itself."
1.18–1.22
Asamprajñāta Samādhi and the Five Qualities Required
Super-conscious samādhi
›
विरामप्रत्ययाभ्यासपूर्वः संस्कारशेषोऽन्यः
virāma-pratyaya-abhyāsa-pūrvaḥ saṃskāra-śeṣo'nyaḥ
Translation: The other samādhi (asamprajñāta) is preceded by the practice of the cessation-intention; only latent impressions (saṃskāras) remain.
What makes asamprajñāta different: No active cognition, no bliss experience, no I-sense — only saṃskāras remain as bare potential, not active modification. This is not unconsciousness — it is the most intense wakefulness without any object. The Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad calls this turīya-adjacent: the state closest to pure awareness before the last veil lifts. Sadasiva Brahmendra himself inhabited this state on the Kāverī's banks — body found apparently lifeless for extended periods.
Sūtra 1.20 — Five qualities for asamprajñāta
श्रद्धावीर्यस्मृतिसमाधिप्रज्ञापूर्वक इतरेषाम्
śraddhā-vīrya-smṛti-samādhi-prajñā-pūrvaka itareṣām
Translation: For others, it is preceded by faith, energy, memory, samādhi, and wisdom.
Five qualities
Śraddhā through Prajñā — the inner equipment required
›
QualityIASTYoga Sudhākara meaning
श्रद्धाśraddhāFaith — not blind belief but living conviction from recognizing the teaching resonates with direct experience
वीर्यvīryaEnergy/heroism — sustained effort that does not flag when the path becomes subtle
स्मृतिsmṛtiMemory — continuous recollection of the goal; not forgetting what one is practicing toward
समाधिsamādhiThe one-pointedness already established in samprajñāta stages
प्रज्ञाprajñāWisdom — discriminative insight born from samādhi, not from study alone
These five are interdependent: without śraddhā, vīrya cannot sustain; without vīrya, smṛti lapses; without smṛti, samādhi fragments; without samādhi, prajñā remains theoretical. The sequence is also a diagnostic: wherever the practice stalls, locate which of the five is weak.
Unit 4 · Sūtras 1.23–1.32 · Īśvara Praṇidhāna and Oṃkāra
Unit insight
Patañjali offers an alternative path for those who cannot yet directly practice the samādhi stages: surrender to Īśvara through Oṃkāra. Sadasiva Brahmendra's Advaitic reading makes Īśvara not an external being but the ground consciousness — making surrender the dropping of the false self that imagined itself separate.
1.24–1.26
Īśvara — The Untouched Puruṣa
Nature of the Lord
›
क्लेशकर्मविपाकाशयैरपरामृष्टः पुरुषविशेष ईश्वरः
kleśa-karma-vipāka-āśayair aparāmṛṣṭaḥ puruṣa-viśeṣa īśvaraḥ
Word by word
SanskritIASTMeaning
क्लेशkleśaafflictions (avidyā, asmitā, rāga, dveṣa, abhiniveśa)
कर्मkarmaaction and accumulated impressions
विपाकvipākafruits/results of karma
अपरामृष्टःaparāmṛṣṭaḥuntouched; uncontaminated — never entered into karma's field
पुरुषविशेषpuruṣa-viśeṣaa special Puruṣa — distinguished by being eternally free, never bound
Translation: Īśvara is a special Puruṣa untouched by afflictions, actions, their fruits, and latent impressions.
Yoga Sudhākara — the Advaitic Īśvara: Sadasiva Brahmendra's decisive move: Īśvara is not a separate being outside the seeker — he is the ground Puruṣa, the consciousness that was never contaminated because it was never actually within time, action, or matter. The difference between Īśvara and the jīva (individual soul) is not metaphysical — it is epistemic. Īśvara knows itself as Puruṣa; the jīva forgets. Praṇidhāna (surrender) is therefore the dropping of the false identity, not petition to an external lord. Bhakti and jñāna converge here.
Sūtra 1.26 — Guru of all gurus: Īśvara is sarvajña-bīja (seed of all-knowing), the teacher of even the ancient masters, "not limited by time." Sadasiva Brahmendra connects this to Dakṣiṇāmūrti — Śiva teaching in silence. His teaching is not words but the direct transmission of the witness-state. This is the metaphysical basis of the guru-śiṣya relationship in this tradition.
1.27–1.29
Praṇava — Oṃkāra as the Expression of Īśvara
Oṃ and its practice
›
तस्य वाचकः प्रणवः।
तज्जपस्तदर्थभावनम्।
ततः प्रत्यक्चेतनाधिगमोऽप्यन्तरायाभावश्च॥
tasya vācakaḥ praṇavaḥ |
taj-japas tad-artha-bhāvanam |
tataḥ pratyak-cetanā-adhigamo'py antarāya-abhāvaś ca ||
1.27: His expression is the Praṇava (Oṃ).
1.28: Its repetition with contemplation of its meaning [is prescribed].
1.29: From this, inward-turned consciousness arises, and obstacles disappear.
Oṃkāra structure
Māṇḍūkya framework — the four levels of Oṃ
›
SoundStateConsciousness level
अ (a)Waking (jāgrat)Viśva — gross body, outward awareness
उ (u)Dream (svapna)Taijasa — subtle body, dream consciousness
म (m)Deep sleep (suṣupti)Prājña — causal body, undifferentiated bliss

Sadasiva Brahmendra: repetition of Oṃ with artha-bhāvanā (contemplation of this meaning) is not mere mantra-japa — it is a meditation on the complete structure of consciousness. Each cycle of Oṃ traces the journey from gross outward awareness through dream and sleep to the silent witness. The key term in 1.29 is pratyak-cetanā — inward-turned consciousness. This reversal of the outward-flowing mind is not created by the japa; it is the natural direction of consciousness when the outward craving relaxes. Oṃ is the vehicle; the silence after Oṃ is the destination.
1.30–1.32
Nine Obstacles and the Universal Remedy
Obstacles to yoga
›
व्याधिस्त्यानसंशयप्रमादालस्याविरतिभ्रान्तिदर्शनालब्धभूमिकत्वानवस्थितत्वानि चित्तविक्षेपास्तेऽन्तरायाः
vyādhi-styāna-saṃśaya-pramāda-ālasya-avirati-bhrānti-darśana-alabdha-bhūmikatva-anavasthitatvāni citta-vikṣepās te'ntarāyāḥ
Translation: Disease, dullness, doubt, negligence, laziness, non-abstinence, false perception, non-attainment of a stage, instability — these are the nine obstacles, distractors of the mind.
ObstacleIASTWhat it actually is
व्याधिvyādhiPhysical illness — body interrupts meditation; solved by āsana, diet, prāṇāyāma
स्त्यानstyānaDullness — tamas crystallized; the mind knows the path but will not move
संशयsaṃśayaDoubt — especially doubt of the path; self-reinforcing and most dangerous
प्रमादpramādaNegligence — knowing what to do but not doing it; heedlessness
आलस्यālasyaLaziness — heaviness; body and mind resist the effort
अविरतिaviratiNon-abstinence — craving for sense-objects persists despite practice
भ्रान्तिदर्शनbhrānti-darśanaFalse perception — delusion about one's actual level of attainment

Sūtra 1.32 — The single remedy: Eka-tattva-abhyāsa — practice of one principle. Rather than attacking each obstacle separately, Sadasiva Brahmendra's remedy is radical simplicity: return to a single pointed practice. One mantra. One meditation object. One teaching from the guru. When the mind is fragmented, the remedy is not analysis of the fragmentation but return to unity. This principle — simplicity over complexity when scattered — is one of the most practically useful teachings in the entire text.
Unit 5 · Sūtras 1.33–1.51 · Stabilizing Practices and Nirbīja Samādhi
Unit insight
Seven practices to stabilize the citta, followed by Patañjali's most precise technical description of how samādhi deepens through paired stages to the seedless, objectless absorption that is the threshold of Kaivalya. The crystal analogy (1.41) is Sadasiva Brahmendra's most quoted teaching in this chapter.
1.33–1.39
Seven Practices to Stabilize the Citta
Mind-clearing methods
›
मैत्रीकरुणामुदितोपेक्षाणां सुखदुःखपुण्यापुण्यविषयाणां भावनातश्चित्तप्रसादनम्
maitrī-karuṇā-muditā-upekṣāṇāṃ sukha-duḥkha-puṇya-apuṇya-viṣayāṇāṃ bhāvanātaś citta-prasādanam
1.33: The mind becomes clear by cultivating friendliness (maitrī) toward the happy, compassion (karuṇā) toward the suffering, joy (muditā) toward the virtuous, and equanimity (upekṣā) toward the non-virtuous.
Key insight — attitudes as technology, not morality: Sadasiva Brahmendra reads these four attitudes not as ethical prescriptions but as mind-calming mechanisms. Envy when seeing others' happiness creates agitation (rajas). Compassion instead of despair when seeing suffering keeps sattva intact. The four attitudes are chosen specifically because they are the four most common occasions for citta-disturbance in social life. This is practical psychology of citta-hygiene, not virtue ethics.

1.38Svapna-nidrāUsing dream and sleep states as meditation support — borderline states are accessible
1.39YathābhimataWhatever is agreeable to the seeker — any effective object of concentration
Sadasiva Brahmendra's principle: any of the seven will work if pursued with abhyāsa and vairāgya together. The path is pluralistic in method, singular in destination. The seventh (yathābhimata) is the most permissive — Patañjali explicitly allows the seeker's own temperament to guide the choice of practice.
1.40–1.45
Samāpatti — Crystal-like Absorption
The coalescence teaching
›
क्षीणवृत्तेरभिजातस्येव मणेर्ग्रहीतृग्रहणग्राह्येषु तत्स्थतदञ्जनता समापत्तिः
kṣīṇa-vṛtter abhijātasyeva maṇer grahītṛ-grahaṇa-grāhyeṣu tat-stha-tad-añjanatā samāpattiḥ
Translation: When modifications are diminished, the mind becomes like a transparent crystal — taking on the color of whatever is near it (the knower, the knowing, the known). This is samāpatti (coalescence).
Yoga Sudhākara — the crystal teaching: The mind in samāpatti is like a crystal placed on a red surface — it appears red, but its own crystalline nature is unaltered. The mind "becomes" the object without being it, without pure consciousness itself being modified. This is Sadasiva Brahmendra's most precise statement on the samādhi paradox. When samāpatti is applied to the final object — Puruṣa itself — the crystal cognizes its own luminosity. There is no separate object. The knower, knowing, and known collapse into one. This is the threshold of Kaivalya.
"In samāpatti, the meditator vanishes into the meditation. This is not a problem — it is the goal. The 'you' that feared vanishing was the veil, not the Seer."
1.46–1.51
Nirbīja Samādhi — The Seedless State
Final samādhi of Ch.I
›
तस्यापि निरोधे सर्वनिरोधान्निर्बीजः समाधिः
tasyāpi nirodhe sarva-nirodhān nirbījaḥ samādhiḥ
Translation: When even this (the impression of samādhi itself) is restrained, seedless samādhi arises from complete cessation of all.
The final dissolution: Sabīja samādhi leaves an impression — "I was in samādhi." This impression (saṃskāra) is itself a seed. While a seed remains, re-germination into saṃsāra is possible. Nirbīja is when the last seed is destroyed at its root — not suppressed but roasted, incapable of sprouting again. Sadasiva Brahmendra's Advaitic equivalence: nirbīja samādhi = Kaivalya in Yoga language = Brahman-realization in Vedānta language = turīya in Upaniṣadic language. These are not different states described in different traditions — they are one recognition expressed in four vocabularies.
Ṛtambharā prajñā
Sūtras 1.48–1.50 — Truth-bearing wisdom
›
ऋतम्भरा तत्र प्रज्ञा
ṛtambharā tatra prajñā
Translation: There (in nirvicāra samādhi), the wisdom is ṛtambharā — truth-bearing.
Ṛtambharā prajñā is the wisdom that arises from direct samādhi — not inference, not scripture, not reasoning. It is described as viśeṣārthatvāt (having a specific, unique content) distinguished from ordinary knowledge. This wisdom directly contradicts the accumulated saṃskāras of ignorance — and the saṃskāra born from it overpowers all previous saṃskāras. This is the penultimate stage before nirbīja: wisdom strong enough to uproot its own root.
"Nirbīja is not an experience. It is the final recognition that there was never a seed — the Seer was always already seedless, timeless, untouched. The seeking ends not because it succeeded but because it is seen through."

02

YS 2.3–2.9
›
Class focus: The five kleśas as the root cause of all suffering. Sadasiva Brahmendra's commentary places avidyā (ignorance) not as one kleśa among five but as the ground from which all four others grow — making its removal the singular task of the entire yogic path.

The Swami: this is where Yoga and Advaita merge completely — the sole remedy in both is discrimination.
03
Karma, Suffering, and Viveka-Khyāti
YS 2.12–2.27
›
Class focus: How kleśas generate karmic accumulation, how all experience is ultimately painful for the discriminating intelligence, and how viveka-khyāti (unbroken discriminative awareness) is the only means of liberation.
Sūtra 2.15 — All is Suffering (for the discriminating)
परिणामतापसंस्कारदुःखैर्गुणवृत्तिविरोधाच्च दुःखमेव सर्वं विवेकिनः

Translation: For the discriminating, everything is indeed suffering — due to the pain of change, anxiety, latent impressions, and the conflict of the guṇas.
Yoga Sudhākara: Sadasiva Brahmendra does not read this as pessimism but as precision. The Swami: only the one who has tasted the bliss of samādhi — even briefly — recognizes that all ordinary experience is tinged with the subtle pain of impermanence. This recognition is not depressing; it is liberating. It is the force that drives the seeker from the peripheral enjoyments of vikṣipta mind to the steady focus required for samādhi.
Sūtra 2.26 — The Remedy
विवेकख्यातिरविप्लवा हानोपायः
viveka-khyātir aviplavā hāna-upāyaḥ
Translation: Uninterrupted discriminative awareness is the means of cessation [of suffering].
Yoga Sudhākara — the key word aviplavā: "Uninterrupted" — not occasional flashes of discrimination but the continuous, steady background recognition of the distinction between Puruṣa and Prakṛti (or Ātman and the world). Sadasiva Brahmendra: this unbroken quality is what makes it a means of liberation rather than just philosophy. The Swami: this is why practice must be dīrgha-kāla (long-term) and nairantarya (uninterrupted) as in YS 1.14 — because an interrupted discrimination allows the kleśas to regrow.

Class focus: The last three outer limbs. Sadasiva Brahmendra's treatment of āsana is minimal — the commentary is deliberately brief because the Yoga Sudhākara is oriented toward the inner limbs. His prāṇāyāma commentary, however, is detailed and contains stages not found in Vyāsa.

Yoga Sudhākara — detailed prāṇāyāma commentary: Sadasiva Brahmendra identifies three aspects of prāṇāyāma: external (bāhya), internal (ābhyantara), and restrained (stambha-vṛtti). He adds a fourth — bāhyābhyantara-viṣaya-ākṣepī — the prāṇāyāma that transcends the inner/outer distinction entirely. This fourth is the bridge to dhāraṇā. The Swami explains: this is the prāṇāyāma of the avadhūta — not controlled breathing but the natural pause of prāṇa in deep samādhi, described by Sadasiva Brahmendra from his own experience.
Unit 3 · Aṣṭāṅga continued — Inner Limbs preview
The inner three limbs (dhāraṇā, dhyāna, samādhi) are introduced here and treated fully in Chapter III. Sadasiva Brahmendra's commentary at the end of Chapter II points forward — the outer limbs have prepared the vessel; now the inner limbs fill it.
"The outer limbs are the banks of the river. The inner limbs are the river itself. Without banks, water spreads and becomes swamp. Without water, banks are merely walls of dried earth."
— Yoga Sudhākara teaching, as rendered by Swami Paramahamsananda Sarasvatī

Chapter theme

Kaivalya Pāda

कैवल्यपादः · Chapter IV · 34 Sūtras

Chapter theme — Final resolution
पुरुषार्थशून्यानां गुणानां प्रतिप्रसवः कैवल्यम्
Kaivalya is the return of the guṇas to their origin when they are empty of purpose for the Puruṣa — or the establishment of consciousness in its own nature. This is the goal toward which all four chapters have been moving.
01
Sources of Powers and the Creation of Multiple Minds
YS 4.1–4.11
›
Class focus: The chapter opens by returning to siddhis — but now to explain their deeper metaphysics. Sadasiva Brahmendra expands significantly here on the role of Prakṛti in creating the illusion of multiple minds.
Sūtra 4.1 — Five sources of powers
जन्मौषधिमन्त्रतपःसमाधिजाः सिद्धयः
janma-auṣadhi-mantra-tapaḥ-samādhi-jāḥ siddhayaḥ
Translation: Powers arise from birth, herbs, mantra, austerity, and samādhi.
Yoga Sudhākara: Sadasiva Brahmendra notes that only samādhi-born siddhis are truly stable — the others are temporary and can become obstacles because they arise without the corresponding purification of the citta. The Swami: this explains why Sadasiva Brahmendra himself could manifest powers while remaining unattached — they were samādhi-born, arising from the natural fullness of realized consciousness, not from effort or craving.
02
The Mind, Consciousness, and the Nature of Objects
YS 4.12–4.24
›
Class focus: Patañjali's metaphysics of time, objects, and the relationship between pure consciousness and the mind. Sadasiva Brahmendra's commentary here is explicitly Advaitic, drawing extensively on Śaṅkara's analysis of adhyāsa (superimposition).
Sūtra 4.18 — Consciousness is always the knower of the mind
सदा ज्ञाताश्चित्तवृत्तयस्तत्प्रभोः पुरुषस्यापरिणामित्वात्
sadā jñātāś citta-vṛttayas tat-prabhoḥ puruṣasya apariṇāmitvāt
Translation: The modifications of the mind are always known to their master, Puruṣa, because of Puruṣa's unchangeable nature.
Yoga Sudhākara — the unchangeable witness: This sūtra is Sadasiva Brahmendra's proof-text for the witness-consciousness teaching. Puruṣa knows all modifications of the mind because it is apariṇāmī — unchanging. Only the unchanging can be the stable ground of knowledge. The changing mind cannot know itself — it requires the unchanging witness. The Swami: this is precisely the Advaitic teaching of the sākṣin (witness). Sadasiva Brahmendra reads Patañjali's Puruṣa as functionally identical to Śaṅkara's Sākṣin-Ātman.
03

YS 4.25–4.32

Translation: For one who has no interest even in the highest discriminative knowledge, dharmamegha (cloud of virtue/dharma) samādhi arises from uninterrupted viveka-khyāti.
Yoga Sudhākara — the penultimate state: Sadasiva Brahmendra: dharmamegha samādhi is the state just before Kaivalya. "Cloud of dharma" — virtue rains down spontaneously, the fruits of all prior practice pour out, and the kleśas and karmas are destroyed at their root. The Swami: this is the state Sadasiva Brahmendra inhabited near the end of his earthly life — from which he would fall into extended samādhi on the Kāverī's banks, his body found apparently lifeless, reviving later. Not performance, not attainment — simply the natural overflow of a consciousness that had dissolved its last obstruction.
04
Kaivalya — Absolute Freedom
YS 4.33–4.34
›
Class focus: The final two sūtras of the Yoga Sutras — and Sadasiva Brahmendra's climactic commentary reading Kaivalya as non-different from Brahman-realization. This is the distinctive signature of the Yoga Sudhākara.
Sūtra 4.33 — Krama (sequence) ends
क्षणप्रतियोगी परिणामापरान्तनिर्ग्राह्यः क्रमः

Translation: Sequence is the succession of moments, recognized at the end of a transformation.
Yoga Sudhākara: Sadasiva Brahmendra: in Kaivalya, sequence (krama) ceases. The yogī lives in the eternal now — the sukṣmaṃ of pure consciousness where past, present, and future are equally known without succession. This is the technical basis for Sadasiva Brahmendra's known bilocation — from outside krama, presence is not limited to one body in one place.
Sūtra 4.34 — Kaivalya
पुरुषार्थशून्यानां गुणानां प्रतिप्रसवः कैवल्यं स्वरूपप्रतिष्ठा वा चितिशक्तिरिति
puruṣa-artha-śūnyānāṃ guṇānāṃ pratiprasavaḥ kaivalyaṃ svarūpa-pratiṣṭhā vā citi-śaktir iti
Word by word
SanskritIASTMeaning
पुरुषार्थशून्यानाम्puruṣa-artha-śūnyānāmempty of purpose for Puruṣa; guṇas having no more work to do
गुणानाम्guṇānāmof the guṇas (sattva, rajas, tamas)
प्रतिप्रसवःpratiprasavaḥreturn to origin; involution; counter-movement back to the source
कैवल्यम्kaivalyamaloneness; absolute freedom; liberation (kevala = alone)
स्वरूपप्रतिष्ठाsvarūpa-pratiṣṭhāestablishment in one's own nature; abiding as the Self
चितिशक्तिःciti-śaktiḥthe power of consciousness; pure awareness as the final ground
Translation: Kaivalya is the return of the guṇas to their origin when they are empty of purpose for Puruṣa — or the establishment of citi-śakti (pure consciousness-power) in its own nature.
Yoga Sudhākara — the final commentary: Sadasiva Brahmendra's decisive Advaitic reading: the phrase "svarūpa-pratiṣṭhā vā citi-śaktiḥ" — "or the establishment of the power of consciousness in its own nature" — is not an alternative definition but the deeper definition. Kaivalya is not the isolation of Puruṣa from Prakṛti (as classical Sāṃkhya-Yoga holds) but the recognition of citi-śakti as the only reality. Prakṛti was never separate — it was always an appearance within consciousness. When this is directly known, guṇas do not "return" anywhere — they are seen as they always were: movements within the ocean of pure awareness. The Swami: this is the complete equation — Kaivalya = Brahman-realization = nirbīja samādhi = viveka-khyāti fully matured. All four chapters end at the same place, approached from different angles.
"Kaivalya is not an achievement. It is the recognition that the one who was seeking liberation was already the only reality. The guṇas cease their activity not because they are destroyed, but because the one for whose sake they arose is recognized as having never been bound."
— Yoga Sudhākara, final commentary, as rendered by Swami Paramahamsananda Sarasvatī
Course closing mahāsūtra
योगश्चित्तवृत्तिनिरोधः — तदा द्रष्टुः स्वरूपेऽवस्थानम्
Yoga is the cessation of the modifications of the mind — then the Seer abides in its own nature. This is the beginning and the end. The first sūtra contains the last. The last illuminates the first. The entire teaching is a circle — enter anywhere, arrive at the same point.
