Sections
Opening Question · The Root Inquiry
केनेषितं पतति प्रेषितं मनः
"By whom directed does the mind fly forth? By whom commanded does the breath go first?" — Kena 1.1 · The text opens with the most radical question in philosophy: what is the witness behind all witnessing?

Ādi Śaṅkarācārya — Bhāṣya on Kena

Śaṅkara's commentary (Kenopaniṣad Bhāṣya) remains the definitive Advaita reading. He wrote two commentaries: one on the Padabhāṣya (word-by-word, mantra section) and one on the Vākyabhāṣya (sentence, prose section). His core argument: Brahman cannot be the object of any cognition — it is the very subject that makes all cognition possible. The Upaniṣad's radical claim is that Brahman is known precisely by NOT being known as an object.

Swami Chinmayananda — Modern Exposition

Chinmayananda's talks and written commentary (published by Chinmaya Mission) are among the most accessible modern treatments. His approach: the Kena is the "Upaniṣad of Humility" — all spiritual powers, all ego-functions, all senses operate only by the light of Brahman they cannot see. The Yakṣa parable is treated as a direct prescription for the spiritual life: the devout ego must be humbled before true Knowledge dawns.

Swami Vivekananda · Swami Gambhirananda

Vivekananda's lectures on Kena (Complete Works, Vol. 2) emphasize its universal message: the Infinite cannot be grasped by the finite. Gambhirananda's translation (Advaita Ashrama, 1958) follows Śaṅkara closely and remains the scholarly standard in English, used here alongside S. Radhakrishnan's philosophical notes (The Principal Upanishads, 1953).

What makes Kena distinctive
Structure
Verse + Prose — Two interlocking halves
›
Khaṇḍas I–II (verse): Pure philosophy. Dense, elliptical mantras about the nature of Brahman as pure witnessing consciousness — not graspable by any faculty because it is the ground of all faculties. These mantras demand repeated reading; each word repays sustained attention.

Khaṇḍas III–IV (prose): The Yakṣa parable — a narrative that embodies the philosophy. The gods become arrogant after a cosmic victory. Brahman appears as a mysterious Yakṣa (spirit). One by one Agni, Vāyu, and Indra fail to identify it. Uma Haimavatī reveals the truth to Indra. The narrative is the philosophy in action.
Śaṅkara's reading: the verse section establishes what Brahman IS. The prose section illustrates what happens when that knowledge is absent — pride in the ego's instruments — and how it is recovered: through the grace of Devī (Uma) and the humility to receive it.
Tradition
Sāmaveda · Talavakāra Brāhmaṇa
›
Affiliation: The Kena is the ninth chapter of the Talavakāra Brāhmaṇa (also called Jaiminīya-Upaniṣad-Brāhmaṇa) of the Sāmaveda. It is one of the ten principal Upaniṣads (Daśopaniṣad) on which Śaṅkara composed bhāṣyas — a mark of its supreme canonical status in the Vedāntic tradition.

Name origin: "Kena" = "by whom" — the opening word of the text. Also called Kenopaniṣad or Talavakāropaniṣad.
Among the Daśopaniṣads, Kena is unusual for its dramatic narrative structure in the prose section. Most Upaniṣads with prose parables (like Chāndogya or Bṛhadāraṇyaka) are far longer. Kena's economy — 34 mantras delivering complete Advaita — makes it ideal for intensive study.
Study method
How to use this companion
›
For each mantra: Read the Devanāgarī aloud if possible. Then the IAST transliteration. Then the word-by-word. Then at least two translations (Gambhirananda and one other). Then Śaṅkara's bhāṣya note. Finally, sit with the Contemplation Anchor.

Pace: The verse khaṇḍas (I–II) repay slow reading — one or two mantras per session. The prose khaṇḍas (III–IV) can be read as a continuous narrative, then unpacked mantra by mantra.

Key practice: After reading Kena 1.5–1.9 (the via negativa of Brahman), sit in meditation attempting to locate the "seer behind the seeing." This is the Upaniṣad's direct pointing.

Khaṇḍa I

प्रथमः खण्डः · The Inexpressible Brahman · 8 Mantras

Khaṇḍa I — Theme
नेत्रस्य नेत्रं मनसो मनो यत्
Eye of the eye, mind of the mind — Brahman is the ultimate illuminator of all instruments of knowing. The khaṇḍa proceeds by systematic negation: Brahman is not known by speech, mind, eye, ear, or vital breath — yet these function only by its light.
1.1
The Opening Question — By Whom?
केन इषितम्
›
Mantra
केनेषितं पतति प्रेषितं मनः।
केन प्राणः प्रथमः प्रैति युक्तः।
केनेषितां वाचमिमां वदन्ति।
चक्षुः श्रोत्रं क उ देवो युनक्ति॥
kenéṣitaṃ patati préṣitaṃ manaḥ |

Gambhirananda (Advaita Ashrama): "By whom directed does the mind fly forth? By whom commanded does the first breath go? By whose impulsion do men utter this speech? What effulgent being directs the eye and ear?"
Śaṅkara's Bhāṣya: The disciple comes to the teacher already having performed the preparatory disciplines (śama, dama, etc.). His question is not academic: "I see, I think, I speak — but what am I? Who is behind all these activities?" The genius of mantra 1.1 is that it does not ask "what is God?" but asks about the agent of the seeker's own mental and sensory acts. The inquiry is turned inward from the very first word.
Chinmayananda: The mantra catalogues the four great instruments: mind (thought), prāṇa (life), speech (expression), eye and ear (perception). Each of these is an instrument. An instrument requires an operator. This is the question: who operates the instruments? The answer will be: not another instrument. Not another mind above the mind, another eye above the eye. The operator is of an entirely different order.
Contemplation Anchor
Right now, you are reading. Who is reading? The eyes process light. The mind decodes symbols. But who knows that the reading is happening? Can that knower be seen? Can it be an object of your attention — or does it retreat the moment you look for it?
1.2
The Teacher's Response — Brahman Is That
श्रोत्रस्य श्रोत्रम्

Mantra

चक्षुषश्चक्षुरतिमुच्य धीराः

Radhakrishnan (The Principal Upanishads): "That which is not thought by the mind, but by which, they say, the mind is thought — know that alone as Brahman, not this which people worship here."

Gambhirananda: "It is the ear of the ear, the mind of the mind, the speech of speech, the life of the life, and the eye of the eye. Having detached from the senses and renouncing this world, the wise become immortal."
Śaṅkara's key insight: The phrase "ear of the ear" does not mean a second, inner ear above the physical ear. It means the consciousness (cit) that illuminates all sensory experience. The ear hears — but it cannot hear itself hearing. The mind thinks — but cannot think the thinker. Brahman is precisely that which makes all these operations possible while remaining beyond their reach. To "know" Brahman is to recognise that you already ARE this knowing awareness.
Vivekananda's formulation: We are always knowing Brahman — there is nothing else to know. What we mistake is taking Brahman to be an object. The moment the mind turns back on itself completely, what remains is Brahman. This mantra is the philosophical core of the entire Kena Upaniṣad.
Contemplation Anchor
"Ear of the ear, mind of the mind." The eye cannot see itself seeing. The mind cannot think the thinker. Brahman is what remains when every object of knowledge is exhausted. It is not found — it is what finds.
1.3
Not Knowable as Object — The First Paradox
न तत्र चक्षुर्गच्छति
›
Mantra
न तत्र चक्षुर्गच्छति न वाग्गच्छति नो मनः।
न विद्मो न विजानीमो यथैतदनुशिष्यात्॥
na tatra cakṣur gacchati na vāg gacchati no manaḥ |
na vidmo na vijānīmo yathaitad anuśiṣyāt ||
Gambhirananda: "The eye does not go there, nor speech, nor mind. We do not know, we do not understand how anyone can teach this."
Śaṅkara: The teacher himself says "we do not know" — but this is not agnosticism. The "not knowing" here is the highest form of knowledge: knowledge that Brahman cannot be objectified. To "not know" Brahman as an object is the beginning of knowing it as oneself. The teacher teaches by communicating the impossibility of teaching — which is itself the teaching.
Chinmayananda: The verse "na tatra cakṣur gacchati" — "the eye does not go there" — is the Upaniṣad's famous via negativa. Brahman is not found by looking outward, nor by memory, nor by inference. This progressively strips away all avenues of conventional knowledge, leaving the student with only awareness itself as the remaining "object" — which is precisely where Brahman is "found."
Contemplation Anchor
Sit quietly. Don't look for anything. Don't think any thought about Brahman. Don't try to feel it. What is the awareness that notices this very attempt and its failure?
1.4
What Is Unknown to the Knowers
अन्यदेव तद्विदितात्
›
Mantra
अन्यदेव तद्विदितादथो अविदितादधि।
इति शुश्रुम पूर्वेषां ये नस्तद्व्याचचक्षिरे॥
anyad eva tad viditād atho aviditād adhi |
iti śuśruma pūrveṣāṃ ye nas tad vyācacakṣire ||
Gambhirananda: "It is indeed other than the known, and again, it is above the unknown. Thus did we hear from the ancients who explained it to us."
Śaṅkara: Brahman is "other than the known" (viditāt) because the known is an object — Brahman is never an object. It is also "beyond the unknown" (aviditāt) — Brahman is not simply ignorance or void. It is the luminous ground on which both knowledge and ignorance appear. This mantra kills two possible misconceptions: (1) that Brahman is a subtle object you could eventually perceive, and (2) that Brahman is the same as nothing or darkness.
Contemplation Anchor
Not an object you can grasp. Not a void. Not the known. Not the unknown. What is left? Only the very awareness in which "known" and "unknown" arise as categories.
1.5–1.8
The Four Paradoxes — Known by Not-Knowing
यस्यामतं तस्य मतम्
›
Mantras 1.5 & 1.6
यस्यामतं तस्य मतं मतं यस्य न वेद सः।
अविज्ञातं विजानतां विज्ञातमविजानताम्॥

प्रतिबोधविदितं मतममृतत्वं हि विन्दते।
आत्मना विन्दते वीर्यं विद्यया विन्दतेऽमृतम्॥
yasyāmataṃ tasya mataṃ mataṃ yasya na veda saḥ |
avijñātaṃ vijānatāṃ vijñātam avijānatām ||

pratibodha-viditaṃ matam amṛtatvaṃ hi vindate |
ātmanā vindate vīryaṃ vidyayā vindate'mṛtam ||
1.5 — Radhakrishnan: "He by whom It [Brahman] is not thought, by him It is thought; he by whom It is thought, knows It not. It is not understood by those who understand It; It is understood by those who do not understand It."

1.6 — Gambhirananda: "It is known in every state of consciousness — it is thereby considered known as the ground of immortality. Through the Self one finds strength; through Knowledge one finds immortality."
Śaṅkara on 1.5 — The great paradox: "He who thinks he knows Brahman, knows it not" — because knowing Brahman as an object is not knowing Brahman. "He by whom it is not thought" — the one who has given up the effort to make Brahman an object of thought, and rests in pure awareness — has the true "knowledge." This is the most quoted verse of the Kena, often compared with Socrates' "I know that I know nothing."

On 1.6 — pratibodha-viditam: "Known in each cognition." Brahman is the witness present in every perception, every thought, every moment of consciousness. You have never NOT been in the presence of Brahman. It is the very fact of awareness itself — the "that it is" of every experience.
Mantras 1.7 & 1.8
इह चेदवेदीदथ सत्यमस्ति
न चेदिहावेदीन्महती विनष्टिः।
भूतेषु भूतेषु विचित्य धीराः
प्रेत्यास्माल्लोकादमृता भवन्ति॥
iha ced avedīd atha satyam asti
na ced ihāvedīn mahatī vinaṣṭiḥ |
bhūteṣu bhūteṣu vicitya dhīrāḥ
pretyāsmāl lokād amṛtā bhavanti ||
1.7 — Gambhirananda: "If one has known It here, then there is truth; if one has not known It here, then there is great destruction. The wise, discerning It in all beings, become immortal on departing from this world."
Chinmayananda on 1.7: "iha ced avedīt" — if one realises in this very lifetime, here, in this embodied existence — then there is truth, then life has meaning. The urgency is remarkable: there is no deferral. "The wise, discerning It in all beings" — the realised one sees the one consciousness that underlies all differentiated existence. This is not a poetic metaphor; it is the direct description of what Self-realisation looks like from within.
Contemplation Anchor
"Known in every state of consciousness." Not only in deep meditation. Not only in silence. Right now, in this moment of reading — what is it that is always already present, that does not need to be achieved?

Khaṇḍa II

द्वितीयः खण्डः · That Which Is Not Known · 5 Mantras

Khaṇḍa II — Theme
न विद्यो न विजानीमः
Khaṇḍa II deepens the paradox of Khaṇḍa I. If Brahman cannot be known as an object, what is the nature of the "knowing" that is available? The answer: Brahman is apprehended in each flash of cognition as its very substratum — not as a result of seeking, but as the ground of the seeking itself.
2.1–2.2
Brahman Is Not Thought — The Teacher's Own Uncertainty
मन्ये विदितम्
›
Mantras
मन्ये विदितमिति नो न विदितं परं मतम्।
न विदितमिति वेद तद्विदितमविदितम्॥

यस्यामतं तस्य मतं मतं यस्य न वेद सः।
अविज्ञातं विजानतां विज्ञातमविजानताम्॥
manye viditam iti no na viditaṃ paraṃ matam |
na viditam iti veda tad viditam aviditam ||

yasyāmataṃ tasya mataṃ mataṃ yasya na veda saḥ |
avijñātaṃ vijānatāṃ vijñātam avijānatām ||
2.1 — Gambhirananda: "I do not think I know It well, nor do I think that I do not know It. He among us who knows this [that It is neither known nor unknown] — he knows It; he among us who does not know this — he does not know it."

2.2 — Radhakrishnan: "He by whom It is not thought, by him It is thought; he who thinks he knows It knows It not."
Śaṅkara on Mantra 2.1: The teacher now speaks in the first person. "Manye viditam" — "I think I know It." But then the teacher corrects: "neti" — "not thus." This self-correction is the very teaching. The teacher models the renunciation of claiming to have Brahman as an object of knowledge. The true knower is one who knows that Brahman transcends the knower-known structure — and thereby recognises that the very "not-knowing" of Brahman is the highest proximity to it.
Chinmayananda — The Double Negation: Those who claim to know Brahman (as an object they have attained) — they do not know. Those who claim complete ignorance of Brahman (as a thing entirely outside experience) — they also do not know. The one who knows NEITHER of these positions — who maintains the impossible middle: "It is beyond known and unknown" — that one is closest to the truth. This double negation is the hallmark of genuine Vedāntic knowledge.
Contemplation Anchor
Can you genuinely not-know Brahman — not as defeat, but as the most precise and honest acknowledgment of what Brahman is? Resting in not-knowing without the restlessness of seeking — is that the closest the mind can come?
2.3–2.5
Brahman Known in Each Flash of Cognition
प्रतिबोधविदितम्
›
Mantras
तस्यैष आदेशो यद्विद्युत्
व्यद्युतदा इतीन्न्यमीमिषदा इत्यधिदैवतम्॥

अथाध्यात्मं यदेतद्गच्छतीव च मनोऽनेन
चैतदुपस्मरत्यभीक्ष्णं सङ्कल्पः॥

तद्धा तद्वनं नाम तद्वनमित्युपासितव्यम्
स य एतदेवं वेदाभि हैनं सर्वाणि भूतानि संवाञ्छन्ति॥
tasyaiṣa ādeśo yad vidyut
vyadyutadā iti nyn miyamīṣadā ity adhidaivatam ||

athādhyātmaṃ yad etad gacchatīva ca mano'nena
caitad upasmaranty abhīkṣṇaṃ saṅkalpaḥ ||

tad dhā tad vanaṃ nāma tad vanam ity upāsitavyam
sa ya etad evaṃ vedābhi hainaṃ sarvāṇi bhūtāni saṃvāñchanti ||
2.3 — Gambhirananda: "Its indication is this: it is like a flash of lightning — blink! Thus Brahman is taught in terms of the divine [daivata] aspect."

2.4: "Then with reference to the Self: it is as if the mind reaches toward it; by this the mind recurrently (saṅkalpa, intention) remembers It."

2.5 — Gambhirananda: "It is truly called Tad Vana (the delight of all). It is to be worshipped as Tad Vana. He who meditates on It thus — all beings will love him."
Śaṅkara — The lightning analogy (2.3): "Like a flash of lightning" (vidyut) — Brahman is indicated not as a continuous object of meditation but as something that is suddenly recognised in each split second of consciousness. A bolt of lightning illuminates the entire landscape instantly, and in that flash the whole is seen. Similarly, Brahman is the illumination already present — recognised in each act of cognition. The "blink" (nyn = iti) marks the instant recognition, then it is gone as objectified memory — but was fully there.
On "Tad Vana" (2.5): The Upaniṣad gives Brahman a devotional name here — Tad Vana, "the delight of That," or "the forest of That" (Śaṅkara reads it as "that in which all beings delight"). This is the Kena's bridge between jñāna and upāsanā: even Brahman beyond all attributes can be approached as the source of all delight, all meaning, all love. "He who knows It thus — all beings will love him" — this is the natural charisma of the jñānī: not acquired, but as the spontaneous radiation of Brahman through a transparent ego.
Contemplation Anchor
"Like a flash of lightning." In this very moment — before the thought about this moment arises — there is a bare awareness. Not "I am aware." Just awareness. The lightning. Then thought covers it again. Practice: notice the gap before the thought.

Khaṇḍa III

तृतीयः खण्डः · The Yakṣa Parable · 12 Mantras

Khaṇḍa III — Narrative Theme
तस्मिन् यक्षे ब्रह्म इति होवाच
The prose section: Brahman wins a victory for the gods. The gods, mistaking the victory as their own achievement, become arrogant. Brahman appears as an unknown Yakṣa. Agni fails. Vāyu fails. Indra, the greatest, approaches — and Uma Haimavatī reveals the secret. The narrative embodies the entire metaphysics of the verse section.
3.1–3.4
The Victory and Agni's Failure
ब्रह्म ह देवेभ्यो विजिग्ये
›
The Narrative Opens
Prose text (3.1): "brahma ha devebhyo vijigye tasya ha brahmaṇo vijaye devā amahīyanta." — "Brahman conquered for the gods. In the victory of Brahman, the gods became elated [with pride]." The gods claim: "Ours indeed is this victory, ours indeed is this greatness." Brahman, knowing their pride, appeared before them as a Yakṣa — a mysterious, unknown being.
Prose text (3.2–3.4) — Agni approaches: The gods sent Agni (Fire) to investigate. Brahman asked: "What power do you have?" Agni replied: "I can burn all that exists on earth." Brahman placed a blade of grass before Agni and said: "Burn this." Agni rushed at it with all his power — and could not burn it. Agni returned, saying "I could not find out what this Yakṣa is."
Śaṅkara's allegory: Agni represents the power of speech and cognitive articulation. The "blade of grass" Agni cannot burn represents even the simplest object of knowledge — which Agni can name, but cannot truly penetrate. The point: all powers of knowledge, even the most refined, operate within Brahman's light and cannot turn that light upon Brahman itself. Agni burns by Brahman's power — without Brahman, there is no fire at all.
Contemplation Anchor
When you speak, describe, analyse, name — you are Agni. All your cognitive power cannot burn the grass of the question "what is awareness itself?" The very act of trying uses the power of the very thing you are trying to locate.
3.5–3.8
Vāyu's Failure — The Breath Cannot Know the Source of Breath
वायुर्वा इत्यब्रवीत्
›
Narrative
Prose text (3.5–3.8): The gods sent Vāyu (Wind). The Yakṣa asked: "What power do you have?" Vāyu said: "I can blow away all that exists on earth." Brahman placed the same blade of grass. Vāyu rushed at it with all his force — and could not move it. Vāyu returned, equally unable to report what the Yakṣa was.
Śaṅkara on Vāyu: Vāyu represents prāṇa — the vital force, breath, life energy. The point is identical to Agni's: all vital functioning operates within Brahman. The breath cannot hold Brahman as an object because the breath is held BY Brahman. The same Upaniṣadic logic as 1.2: "It is the life of life (prāṇasya prāṇaḥ)." The vital force is powerful within the domain of life — but cannot even approach the source of life itself.
Chinmayananda's reading: Agni = cognitive power (knowing faculty). Vāyu = vital power (life faculty). Both fail because both are instruments of Brahman. An instrument cannot investigate its operator. A torch cannot illuminate the sun. The failure of the gods is not their weakness — it is the nature of the relationship between finite instruments and the infinite ground.
3.9–3.12
Indra and Uma Haimavatī — The Revelation
सा ब्रह्मेति होवाच
›
The Climax of the Parable
Prose text (3.9–3.12): The gods sent Indra, the king of gods, the most refined and powerful. But when Indra reached the place where the Yakṣa stood, the Yakṣa had vanished. In the space where the Yakṣa had been, Indra saw a most beautiful woman — Uma Haimavatī (the daughter of Himavat, consort of Śiva), resplendent with golden light. Indra asked her: "What was that Yakṣa?" Uma replied: "It was Brahman. In the victory of Brahman, you were elated with pride. Know: it was Brahman's victory, not yours." And thus Indra came to know Brahman.
Śaṅkara on Uma Haimavatī: Uma is Vidyā — the power of Knowledge itself. She is Haimavatī — daughter of Himalaya, which Śaṅkara glosses as "the abode of great knowledge" (or Śiva's śakti as pure awareness). The gods could not find Brahman through direct effort — the Yakṣa vanished. Brahman cannot be found by seeking. But when Indra arrived in a state of humility (his failure to find the Yakṣa), Brahman appeared in a different form — the gracious teacher, Uma — and revealed itself. This is the Kena's teaching on grace: Brahman is not attained by force but recognized through grace when the ego's pride has been exhausted.
Significance of Indra's partial success: Indra is a step ahead of Agni and Vāyu. He does not ask "what power do I have?" He simply approaches, in humility, to seek. But even Indra cannot find Brahman by seeking — only by receiving Uma's teaching. This models the relationship between inquiry (vicāra) and grace (anugraha): inquiry purifies the ego, but the final recognition comes from Brahman's own self-revelation through the teacher or the guru-śiṣya relationship.
Contemplation Anchor
You have been Agni (using all cognitive power). You have been Vāyu (using all life energy). You have been Indra (approaching with sincere inquiry but still seeking). Now Uma speaks. The Yakṣa was Brahman — and Brahman is what you already are. Can you stop seeking and simply receive this?

Khaṇḍa IV

चतुर्थः खण्डः · Knowledge, Tapas, and the Final Teaching · 9 Mantras

Khaṇḍa IV — Theme
तस्यै तपो दमः कर्मेति प्रतिष्ठा
Khaṇḍa IV delivers the practical teaching: the means of realisation, the nature of the knower of Brahman, and the supreme upāsanā. Brahman-knowledge rests on tapas (austerity), dama (sense-control), and karma (right action) as its foundation — and is expressed as "all the Vedas as its limbs, truth as its abode."
4.1–4.3
Indra Supremely Blessed — The Fruit of Proximity
इन्द्रो मतरिश्वानौ
›
Prose Text
Text: The Upaniṣad explains why Indra excels all other gods: Agni touched the Yakṣa and tried to know it. Vāyu touched the Yakṣa and tried to know it. Indra was the closest — he was approaching the Yakṣa when it vanished. Furthermore, Uma directly gave Indra the teaching. Therefore among these three, Indra is the most blessed — and among all the gods, Indra excels — because he came closest to Brahman and received direct instruction.
Śaṅkara's note on spiritual grades: The gradation Agni → Vāyu → Indra is not about cosmic power but about proximity to self-knowledge. Agni tried to demonstrate his own power (ego of capability). Vāyu tried to demonstrate his power (ego of vitality). Indra approached in inquiry without demonstrating power — and was therefore receptive to Uma's teaching. The capacity to receive (śraddhā, receptivity) is more important than any intrinsic power.
4.4–4.6
The Upadeśa — Brahman Is the Basis of All Powers
तस्यैष आदेशः
›
Mantras
तस्यैष आदेशो यद्विद्युत् व्यद्युतदा इतीन्न्यमीमिषदा इत्यधिदैवतम्॥

अथाध्यात्मं यदेतद्गच्छतीव च मनोऽनेन
चैतदुपस्मरत्यभीक्ष्णं सङ्कल्पः॥
tasyaiṣa ādeśo yad vidyut vyadyutadā iti — ninyan mīmīṣadā iti — adhidaivatam |

athādhyātmaṃ yad etad gacchatīva ca mano'nena caitad upasmaranty abhīkṣṇaṃ saṅkalpaḥ ||
Gambhirananda: "Its teaching is as follows: This is like a flash of lightning — ah! In the opening of the eye it manifests, thus in terms of the divine. Now in terms of the Self: it is as if the mind moves toward it; by this mind one constantly remembers it — through the repeated intention (saṅkalpa)."
Śaṅkara — adhidaivata and adhyātma: Two approaches to Brahman-recognition are given. "Adhidaivata" — through natural phenomena: the lightning flash is the closest natural analogy to the sudden recognition of Brahman — total illumination, instant, then gone from objective experience. "Adhyātma" — through inner experience: the mind seems to "go toward" Brahman in meditation, never quite grasping it, but being led ever inward by the intention (saṅkalpa) to know it. Both paths converge: sudden outer recognition and gradual inner approach point to the same ground.
The saṅkalpa practice: "By repeated intention (saṅkalpa) one remembers It constantly." Saṅkalpa here is not a goal-directed intention but a continuous orientation of the mind toward the ground of awareness. This is upāsanā — the practice of repeatedly turning the mind toward Brahman not as a distant object but as the present substratum. Over time, this orientation becomes the natural resting state of the purified mind.
Contemplation Anchor
Set an intention: "For the next hour, I will repeatedly remember that the awareness in which all my thoughts arise is Brahman." Not as a belief, not as a visualization — just as a remembering. This is saṅkalpa in the Kena's sense.
4.7–4.9
The Fruit and the Foundation — Final Teaching
तस्यै तपो दमः कर्म प्रतिष्ठा
›
Closing Mantras
तस्यै तपो दमः कर्मेति प्रतिष्ठा।
वेदाः सर्वाङ्गानि। सत्यमायतनम्॥

यो वा एतामेवं वेदापहत्य पाप्मानमनन्ते स्वर्गे लोके ज्येये प्रतितिष्ठति प्रतितिष्ठति॥
tasyai tapo damaḥ karmeti pratiṣṭhā |
vedāḥ sarvāṅgāni | satyam āyatanam ||

yo vā etām evaṃ vedāpahatya pāpmānam anante svarge loke jyeye pratitiṣṭhati pratitiṣṭhati ||
4.8 — Gambhirananda: "For It [Brahman-knowledge], austerity (tapas), restraint (dama), and action (karma) are the foundation. The Vedas are its limbs; truth is its abode."

4.9: "He who knows this, having repelled evil, becomes firmly established in the infinite, supreme, blissful world — he becomes firmly established."
Śaṅkara on the triple foundation:
Tapas — not merely physical austerity but the burning away of attachment through endurance. The capacity to bear without disturbance what cannot be avoided.
Dama — restraint of the senses. Not suppression but the redirection of sensory energy toward the inner inquiry.
Karma — right action, duty fulfilled without ego. The Kena puts action as a foundation of knowledge — karma is not opposed to jñāna but prepares the citta for it by removing the grosser kleśas.

"Vedāḥ sarvāṅgāni" — all the Vedas are as the limbs (aṅga) of this knowledge. Meaning: ritual, grammar, philosophy, astronomy — all of Vedic learning is in service of this single recognition. "Satyam āyatanam" — truth is its home, its abode, its resting place.
Chinmayananda on the double repetition "pratitiṣṭhati pratitiṣṭhati": The text says "he becomes established, established" — the repetition in Upaniṣadic style signals emphasis and certainty. This is not future-oriented. "He becomes established in the blissful infinite" — this is describing the immediate reality of the one who knows. The "infinite supreme blissful world" (anante svarge loke) is not a location after death; it is the nature of Brahman itself, recognized here and now. "Having repelled evil (pāpman)" means: the superimposition of finitude upon the infinite has been dissolved.
Contemplation Anchor · The Final Anchor of Kena
Tapas — can you bear the discomfort of not-knowing without filling it with mental constructs?
Dama — can you withdraw the mind from its habitual outward movement, just for this moment?
Karma — have you acted rightly today, without demanding a particular result?

These three are the preparation. The recognition itself is the response of Brahman to this preparation — it cannot be forced. But the preparation can be made now.
